Helios

UX/UI Design / Concept

A conceptual website for becomming better informed about solar power and searching for providers of the technology.
The Basics

Team Members: Al Tran, Phillip Lowery

My Role: Ideation, Research, Style Guide, Wireframes, Coded Prototype

Project Timeline: 2 Weeks

Problem

Research on the subject of alternative energy found that most people showed some level of interest in investing in it for their homes and lives, with the most interest laying in solar power. The greatest barrier indicated to pursuing solar was cost and confusion on where to start looking.

Challenge

Come up with an idea to help address the problem followed by design and development of a prototype of the solution.

The Process

We chose to create the fictitious company, Helios, which would be a sort of hub, or Yelp-type of website for solar power. The group began with some rough brainstorming/ideation to come to a mutual understanding of what we think the problems to solve could be. We started with an audit of other solar energy sites and noting features we should consider for Helios.

Based on our findings and understanding from our first steps we created a survey with Google forms and shared it on social media. The survey's purpose was to gauge interest in alternative energy/solar power and understand how and if people take advantage of the technology, and finally what the barriers to embracing the technology might be.

Based on the survey results we were able to construct a user flow.

Before the user persona and journey were devoloped a style guide was created to so the team could be on the same visual page as we worked on our respective parts of the project.

Using the research and the style guide a user persona and journey was developed.

The Design

From there low-fidelity and high-fidelity wireframes were developed.

When it came time to develop the the high-fidelity wireframes it was decided to make the primary color be the family of orange from the style guide. Traditionally orange is considered an energetic color, as it is (along with yellow) associated with warmth and the sun. The images had a blue hue to them to complement the orange overtones, and the text was made white not only because it had greater contrast, but because white is a color of cleanliness and purity, both of which go well conceptually with the clean connotations of alternative energy.

Key design considerations incorpoarted at this stage of the design include:
  1. The home page featured the cost saving calculator since research showed that the biggest barrier to pursuing solar power was the cost. The thought being that it's interactivity and the usefulness of the calculator would generate interest that would propel users to the rest of the site.
  2. The "reasons to go solar" page was designed to be easy to read and scan quickly. The big headlines are impossible to miss, and if the user wants to take their time they can read the body copy, but they can get what they'd want to know from scanning the page as well.
  3. The "product page" shows solar panel providers and solar products and allows the user to filter by location and product type.
Testing

A coded protoype was produced for usability testing.

View the coded prototype

The prototype had Google Analytics and Hotjar heatmapping code installed.

This helped to understand how users understood the site. We also received feedback via email and direct messages on Slack.

Usability test findings:
  1. The home page bounce rate was 38% (considered excellent by most measures), most users found the home page content compelling enough to click on to another page.
  2. Heatmapping revealed that on the desktop the cost saving calculator was the most clicked on part of the entire prototype. A good amount of activity took place on the solar product page as well, showing an interest in "shopping" around for solar products.
  3. While the heavy use of orange was meant to match up conceptually with energy and solar power, some users said it was "distracting" and were not sure it was working for the overall design.

We took the color feedback and generated other color schemes that were still in line with the desire to use warm colors and blue as a complement.

A page was added to show what would happen when the user clicks the "learn more" button on a product result. The user would be able interact in a number of ways. They can share with others, rate the product, see other reviews, and make it a favorite. Favoriting items necessitates having a user profile feature, which would be a next step item. The top navigation was changed at this stage to reflect profile creation over email sign ups, with the thinking being email opt in could take place settings (the gear icon).

Next Steps
  1. Incorporate a user profile feature that enables users to save products they like for later consideration.
  2. Update the coded prototype's CSS to match the color scheme from the second version of the high-fidelity wireframes.
  3. Code the "product" page and add to the prototype.
  4. Further usability testing to see if the change in colors shows any appreciable difference user behavior.
  5. More analytics and heatmapping to determine how a further developed product section would lead to conversions, which would be defined as clicking on to the websites where the products are offered.
