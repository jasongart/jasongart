Helios

UX/UI Design / Concept

A conceptual website for becoming better informed about solar power and searching for providers of the technology.
The Basics

Team Members: Al Tran, Phillip Lowery

My Role: Ideation, Research, Style Guide, Wireframes, Coded Prototype

Project Timeline: 2 Weeks

Problem

Research on the subject of alternative energy found that most people showed some level of interest in investing in it for their homes and lives, with the most interest laying in solar power. Users indicated the greatest barriers to pursuing solar power were cost and confusion on where to start looking.

Challenge

Come up with an idea to help address the barriers to entry for solar power, followed by design and development of a prototype of the solution.

The Process

The team resolved to create a fictitious company, Helios, which serve as a sort of hub, or Yelp-type of website, dedicated to educating about solar power and marketing products from the industry. We began with some rough brainstorming/ideation to come to a mutual understanding of what we think the problems to solve could be. We started with an audit of other solar energy sites and noting features we should consider for Helios, as well as possible challenges and shortcomings in the existing design.

Based on our findings and understanding from our first steps we created a survey with Google forms and shared it on social media. The survey's purpose was to gauge interest in alternative energy/solar power and understand how and if people take advantage of the technology, and finally what the barriers to embracing the technology might be.

Based on the survey results we were able to construct a user flow that centered on addressing the primary barrier first, cost. From there users are invited to learn more about solar or shop around for solar products, with the flow eventually leading to a product page.

Next, a style guide was created. We continued to iterate on this as the project progressed, often on the fly. However, having this guide allowed the team to remain on the same page visually as we moved on to work independently on our respective roles.

The user persona and user journey aimed to reflect the path a user would take in pursuing solar power, obstacles met along the way, and how Helios could help overcome them.

The Design

The main aim of the rough sketches and low-fidelity wireframes was to figure out what needed to be on the page, and then see how the necessary elements should be arranged. With each part of the site we strived to keep things simple and digestible in order to gain and keep the user's trust.

With the high-fidelity wireframes the design elements, their placement, and what the UI text would be, all came more into focus.

Aesthetically, the team was aiming for a color palette that would engender feelings of energy and warmth while keeping in mind the color should be balanced by the ability of the user to scan and understand the content well enough to move along to the goal of the user flow, the product page.

Feedback from the research was a crucial to determining what users would see and when they would see it:
  1. The home page featured the cost saving calculator since research showed that the biggest barrier to pursuing solar power was the cost. The thought being that it's interactivity and the usefulness of the calculator would generate interest that would propel users to the rest of the site.
  2. The "reasons to go solar" page was designed to be easy to read and scan quickly. The big headlines are impossible to miss, and if the user wants to take their time they can read the body copy, but they can get what they'd want to know from scanning the page as well. While it was positioned to be the next step the design allowed for ease to bypass it and go to the product page.
  3. The "product page" shows both solar panel providers and solar products. The user can filter by location and product type. This was meant to allow more narrow interests in the technology to be explored, if the user so desired, while also realizing accessibility was greater than they might realize with the capability to search for providers on a local level.
Testing

A coded protoype was produced for usability testing.

View the coded prototype

The prototype had Google Analytics and Hotjar heatmapping code installed. This helped to understand how users understood the site. We also received feedback via email and direct messages on Slack.

Usability testing both confirmed design and research and helped set up the project for improvement:
  1. The home page bounce rate was 38% (considered excellent by most measures), most users found the home page content compelling enough to click on to another page.
  2. Heatmapping revealed that on the desktop the cost saving calculator was the most clicked on part of the entire prototype. Despite the product page being a prototype with no actually active links a good amount of clicking took place on that page as well. Confirming, at least preliminarliy, that users had an interest in "shopping" around for solar products if an easy opportunity to do so was put before them.
  3. While the heavy use of orange was meant to match up conceptually with energy and solar power, some users said it was "distracting" and were not sure it was working for the overall design.
  4. The two calls to action on the home page ("get started" "sign up for updates") needed more visual hierarchy, it wasn't clear which action was more important for the user to accomplish.

We took the color feedback and generated other color schemes that were still in line with the desire to use warm colors and blue as a complement. The intended secondary call to action (Sign Up/Log in) was made a different color and given an outline, so while it's visually set apart it's not as striking as if it was a solid block.

A page was added to show what would happen when the user clicks the "learn more" button on a product result. The user would be able interact in a number of ways. They can share with others, rate the product, see other reviews, and make it a favorite. Favoriting items necessitates having a user profile feature, which would be a next step item. The top navigation was changed at this stage to reflect profile creation over email sign ups, with the thinking being email opt-in could take place in user settings. The user could accomplish this by clicking a gear icon or profile dropdown arrow in the top right corner, which would be visible upon login (replacing the "sign up/log in" button).

Next Steps
  1. Incorporate a user profile feature that enables users to save products they like for later consideration.
  2. Update the coded prototype's CSS to match the color scheme from the second version of the high-fidelity wireframes.
  3. Code the "product" page and add to the prototype.
  4. Further usability testing to see if the change in colors shows any appreciable difference user behavior.
  5. More analytics and heatmapping to determine how a further developed product section would lead to conversions, which would be defined as clicking on to the websites where the products are offered.
